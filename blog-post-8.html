Mentoring Is Not Giving Answers

The instinct when someone comes to you with a problem is to solve it. They are stuck, you are not, so you tell them what to do. It feels helpful. It usually is not.

I have been on both sides of this. When I was the one asking, the times I grew the most were almost never the times someone handed me the answer. They were the times someone asked me something I could not immediately respond to, and I had to sit with it.

That discomfort is the actual work.

When you give someone the answer, you solve one problem. When you ask the right question, you give them a tool they will use on the next ten problems without you. Those are not the same thing, and it is worth being clear about which one you are actually there to do.

The hard part is that asking questions instead of answering them feels slower. Someone is in front of you, a little lost, and you could fix it in thirty seconds. Watching them work through it takes five minutes. That slowness is the investment. You are not solving their current problem. You are building their capacity to think through the next one.

The question that changes things

There are a handful of questions I come back to often. What have you already tried? What do you think is actually going on here? If you had to make a call right now, what would it be? These are not tricks. They are just ways of getting someone to use the thinking they already have, before reaching for someone else's.

Most of the time, people already know more than they think. They are not missing information. They are missing confidence, or a structure, or just someone willing to wait while they work it out. Your job in those moments is to be the waiting, not the answer.

That said, there are moments when the most useful thing really is a direct answer. When someone is new, overwhelmed, and genuinely does not have the foundation to reason from, giving them a foothold is kind. The mistake is when that becomes the default, when every conversation ends with you telling them what to do, and they leave knowing more but thinking less.

Good mentoring is not withholding. It is knowing the difference between a gap and a wobble. A gap needs information. A wobble needs a question.

The other thing worth naming is ownership. When someone solves their own problem, even with prompting from you, it is their solution. They believe in it more. They will defend it when things get hard. When you hand them the answer, it stays yours in some quiet way, and the first time it runs into trouble they come back to you. That is not mentoring. That is dependency, and it is easy to build without meaning to.

If you are in a position where people come to you for guidance, pay attention to how your conversations end. If the person leaves knowing what to do but not why, or with your plan instead of their own, something went sideways. The goal is for them to walk away thinking more clearly, not just thinking what you think.

Ask more. Tell less. And when you do tell, make sure you also explain the reasoning, so the next time they can get there without you.

Feedback That Actually Lands

"Great job" is not feedback. Neither is "this needs work." Both feel like something, but neither tells you what to do next.

I have been on the receiving end of both, and I can tell you the vague praise is almost worse. You walk away feeling good for about ten minutes and then the feeling fades because it attached to nothing real. The vague criticism, at least, signals there is a problem. It just leaves you guessing what it is.

Good feedback does three things. It names the specific thing. It explains why that thing mattered. And it arrives close enough to the moment that the person can still picture what they did.

That is the whole framework. Everything else is just practice.

What specific actually means

Specific does not mean long. "The opening slide buried the main result, and I almost missed it" is specific. "The presentation could be clearer" is not. One sentence can be specific. Three paragraphs can still be vague.

When I give feedback now, I try to point to a moment. The second paragraph of your email. The third question you asked in the meeting. The part where the model performance dropped on the validation set. A moment is something the person can find, return to, and examine. A general impression is just an opinion floating in the air.

The same goes for praise. "Your summary in the standup today made it easy for everyone to understand where things stood" is something a person can repeat. "You are a great communicator" is a compliment, not a signal. Compliments are fine. They are just not feedback.

Timing matters more than most people admit. Feedback delivered two weeks after the fact is mostly archaeology. The person is already somewhere else mentally. They have moved on to the next problem, started a new draft, forgotten the context. Useful feedback lives close to the work.

I try to give it within a day or two. If something is significant, I say something the same day. Not in a reactive way, just a quick note or a short conversation. The closer it lands to the moment, the more the person can actually do with it.

There is also a version of this that gets skipped constantly. Positive feedback in the moment. Someone does something well during a meeting, you notice it, you say nothing because it feels like you are interrupting. Then you plan to mention it later and later never comes. That person never hears it. They have no idea what to repeat.

Being kind does not mean softening the point until it disappears. I have seen feedback wrapped in so many qualifiers that the actual observation got lost. "I think maybe, and I could be wrong, but perhaps..." by the time you finish, no one knows what you said.

Kind means respectful. It means you assume the person wants to improve and you treat them that way. It does not mean removing the content. The content is the whole point.

A structure that works: say what you observed, say what effect it had, say what you would suggest instead. That is it. No preamble required. No apology for having an opinion.

Here is the part for whoever is doing the managing. If people on your team are not bringing you their work early, it is usually because feedback has not felt safe. Not safe meaning they worry it will be used against them, or that you will take over, or that asking for input signals weakness. That perception is yours to fix.

The way you fix it is by giving feedback that helps. Consistently. Without punishing someone for the gap you pointed out. Once people see that your feedback makes their work better without making them feel small, they start showing you everything early. That is when the real improvement happens.

Ask for feedback on your own feedback. Genuinely. "Was that useful, or did I miss the point?" That question alone changes the dynamic.

Feedback is a skill. It gets better with practice. But only if you treat it as something you can improve at, not just something you occasionally do.

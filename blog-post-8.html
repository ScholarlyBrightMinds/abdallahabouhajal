Feedback That Actually Lands

"Great job" does almost nothing. Neither does "that could be better." Both feel like feedback, but neither gives the person in front of you anything to hold onto.

I have been on the receiving end of both, and the frustrating thing is that the vague version often comes from someone who genuinely cares. They are not trying to be unhelpful. They just have not been taught what useful feedback actually sounds like.

So here is what I have learned, mostly from getting it wrong first.

Specific feedback is not harsh feedback. That is the confusion that makes people pull their punches. You can be precise and kind at the same time. In fact, precision is one of the kindest things you can offer, because it gives the other person a real shot at improving. "The intro slide was strong, but by slide four I lost the thread of your argument" is kinder than a vague "it was good overall" that leaves the person walking away thinking they have nothing to fix.

Timing matters just as much as the words. Feedback that arrives three weeks after the fact is barely useful. The moment has passed. The person has already moved on, and the correction lands as criticism of something finished rather than as guidance for something ongoing. If you noticed it in the moment, say it close to the moment. Not right in front of a group, but soon, while the context is still alive for both of you.

What it looks like in practice

I try to follow a simple shape. What did I actually observe. What effect did it have. What could look different next time. That is it. No preamble, no sandwiching, no five sentences of setup before the real thing. Just: I noticed this, it landed this way, here is one thing to try.

"I noticed you interrupted the client twice before they finished their sentence. They seemed to pull back after that. Next time, even if you know where they are heading, let them land. It builds more trust than showing you got there first."

That is feedback. It is observable, it is not a character judgment, and it is actionable. The person can actually do something with it tomorrow.

Positive feedback deserves the same specificity. "You were great" is warm but forgettable. "The way you reframed that question in the meeting gave the whole room permission to slow down" tells someone exactly what to do again. People repeat what they understand, not just what they feel good about.

There is also a version of feedback that gets avoided entirely because the relationship feels too important to risk. I understand that instinct. But silence is not safe. Silence just means the person keeps doing the thing and eventually hears it from someone who cares less, or from a consequence that has no words at all.

Withholding feedback is not protecting someone. It is protecting yourself from an uncomfortable conversation.

If you are in a position where people look to you for direction, this is part of the job. Not the performance review once a year. The small, specific, honest thing said close to the moment. That is what actually changes behavior. That is what people remember when they talk about a manager or a mentor who made a difference.

Say the specific thing. Say it kindly. Say it soon.

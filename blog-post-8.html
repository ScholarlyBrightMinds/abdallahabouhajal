Feedback That Actually Lands

"Great job" is not feedback. It is a pat on the back, and there is nothing wrong with a pat on the back, but we should not confuse the two.

Most of the feedback I got early in my career fell into one of two camps. Either it was so positive it told me nothing, or it was critical in a way that left me anxious but gave me no direction. Both felt like the conversation was more for the other person than for me.

I do not think most people are being careless. I think giving real feedback is genuinely hard. It requires you to have been paying attention. It requires a specific example. It requires enough care to think about what the person can actually do with what you are about to say.

That last part matters most. If someone cannot do anything with what you just said, it was not feedback. It was an opinion.

What specific looks like

There is a real difference between "your presentation was a bit unclear" and "the third slide introduced three new terms before defining any of them, and I think that is where people got lost." The second one is useful because the person knows exactly where to go and what to fix. You gave them a map, not just a feeling.

Same thing on the positive side. "You were great in that meeting" is fine. "You held your position when someone pushed back, and that is what kept the conversation from going in circles" is better. Now they know what to repeat.

Specificity is not about being harsh. It is about being honest and useful at the same time.

Timing matters just as much. Feedback delivered three weeks after the fact is mostly just a record of your memory, and memory is imperfect and selective. Deliver it close to the moment, when the context is still fresh for both of you. A two minute conversation the same afternoon is worth more than a thorough debrief a month later.

Kindness is not the same as softness. You can be direct and still treat someone like a person who wants to improve. The framing is simple: what did you observe, what was the effect, and what might they try differently. That is it. No lecture. No history of past mistakes dragged in. No vague gestures toward general attitude. Just this situation, this effect, this idea.

One more thing that often goes unsaid. The best feedback conversations are two-way. After you say what you observed, ask what they were thinking. Sometimes what looked like a mistake was a deliberate choice with a reason behind it. Sometimes they already know what went wrong and just need permission to name it. Either way, you learn something by asking.

If you manage people or mentor anyone, this is part of the job. Not the annual review, not the half-year check-in, not the feedback form. The small, honest, timely conversation after the meeting ends. That is the one that sticks. That is the one people remember years later when they talk about who helped them grow.

Most people are not looking for validation. They are looking for someone paying enough attention to tell them something true.

Pay that kind of attention. Say the specific thing. Say it soon. Say it like you are on the same side, because you are.

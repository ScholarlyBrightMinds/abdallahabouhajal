Mentoring Is Not Advice

The best mentors I have had did not give me answers. They gave me the right question at the right moment, and then got out of the way.

That sounds simple. It is not. When someone comes to you stuck, and you can see the answer from where you are standing, staying quiet long enough to ask a question instead of just telling them takes real discipline.

Most mentoring drifts into advice-giving because advice feels helpful. You have been where they are. You solved a version of this problem three years ago. So you explain what you did, they nod, and they walk away with your solution to their situation. Which is not quite the same thing.

The problem is not that your advice is wrong. The problem is that it is yours. They did not build it. They do not own it. And six months later, when a slightly different version of the problem shows up, they come back to you for the answer again.

That is not mentoring. That is dependency with good intentions.

What questions actually do

A good question puts the person back in the driver's seat. It makes them think out loud, and thinking out loud is how most people find what they already know but have not said yet.

"What have you tried?" is a good start. Not as a test. As a genuine question, because the answer usually contains the next move, buried under frustration.

"What would you do if you could not ask me?" is a harder one. People do not love it in the moment. But it almost always produces something real, because the answer is usually already there and the person just needed permission to trust it.

"What are you most afraid of in this?" is the one most mentors skip entirely. It is uncomfortable. But the fear is almost always the actual problem. The tactical question they asked you is just the surface.

You do not need to ask all three. One good question, asked with genuine curiosity and followed by actual silence, is worth more than a fifteen-minute download of your accumulated wisdom.

Silence is the part that feels wrong. We rush to fill it. But the pause after a question is where the real thinking happens. Let it sit. Count to five if you have to. The person in front of you is working, and your job in that moment is to not interrupt.

None of this means you never share what you know. There are moments where someone needs a concrete piece of information and the kind thing is to just give it to them. Context on how a decision gets made at your organization. A contact they cannot find on their own. A mistake so costly that steering them around it is the right call. Those are real and they matter.

But those moments are narrower than most mentors think. A lot of what gets offered as necessary guidance is actually the mentor's preference dressed up as wisdom. Learn to tell the difference.

The other trap is fixing the problem in real time. Someone shows you what they built and you start editing it in the meeting. You mean well. But now it is your work, not theirs, and you have just taught them that what they make is not quite good enough until you touch it. That lesson sticks in a bad way.

Better to ask what they would change if they had one more pass. Better to point at the thing that is not quite working and let them figure out why. Better to send them back to do it themselves and come back when they are ready.

The goal of mentoring is not a better deliverable this week. It is a more capable person six months from now. Those two things sometimes pull in opposite directions, and you have to know which one you are actually optimizing for.

If you are in a position where people come to you for guidance, pay attention to the ratio in your conversations. How much are you talking versus asking. If you are the one doing most of the work in the conversation, something has gone sideways. The person who should be thinking hardest is the person with the problem, not you.

Ask the question. Wait for the answer. And trust that the person in front of you is more capable than the silence makes them appear.

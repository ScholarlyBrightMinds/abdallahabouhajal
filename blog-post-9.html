Mentoring Is Not Advice

The best mentors I have had did not solve my problems. They asked me questions I could not stop thinking about.

There is a version of mentoring that looks generous but is actually a little selfish. Someone comes to you with a problem and you give them your answer. Clean, fast, satisfying. You get to feel useful. They get to feel helped. And neither of you notices that you just did the hard part for them.

That habit compounds. The person comes back next week with another problem. And the week after. Not because they cannot think, but because you trained them not to have to.

The most useful thing I ever heard in a mentoring conversation was not an answer. It was a pause, and then: what do you think you should do?

That question is uncomfortable. It forces you back into the chair. It says I am not going to take this from you, even though I could. And the first few times someone does it to you, it feels a little like being abandoned. Then you realize it is the opposite.

Good mentoring is not about withholding help. It is about deciding what kind of help actually serves the person in front of you. Sometimes that is a contact. Sometimes it is a reframe. Sometimes it is just sitting with someone while they think out loud and resisting every urge to fill the silence with your own story.

The questions that do the work

There are a few questions I have started reaching for when someone brings me a problem.

What have you already tried? Most people arrive with more thinking done than they let on. This question surfaces it and also tells you immediately how much runway is left.

What would you do if I were not here? This one is almost unfair in how effective it is. Half the time they answer it themselves before they finish the sentence.

What is the version of this that you would be proud of? Not the safe version, not the fast version. The one they would actually want to tell someone about.

None of these are magic. They work because they return ownership. The person leaves the conversation carrying their own problem, just slightly better equipped to deal with it. That is different from leaving with your solution wedged under their arm.

There is also the opposite failure. The mentor who asks questions but is clearly steering toward the answer they already had. The questions are just a hallway they are walking the person down. That is not mentoring either. It is advice in disguise.

Real questions are open. You have to be genuinely curious about where the other person lands. If you already know the right answer and you are just waiting for them to arrive at it, say so. That is honest. The Socratic trap is worse than just telling them directly.

I get this wrong. I still jump in too fast. Someone starts describing a situation and I recognize something similar from three years ago and I am already opening my mouth. The pattern match fires before the listening does. I have had to learn to wait out that first impulse, because what comes after it is usually better.

If you mentor anyone, formally or just because people keep ending up at your desk, one shift is worth trying. Before you answer, ask one question. Just one. See what comes back. More often than not, the person already has the direction. They just need someone to confirm it is okay to trust themselves.

That is the job. Not to be the smartest person in the conversation. To be the one who makes the other person trust their own thinking a little more than they did before they sat down.

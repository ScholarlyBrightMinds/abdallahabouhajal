Mentoring Is Not Advice

The best mentors I have had never gave me the answer. They gave me a question I could not stop thinking about.

There is a difference, and it matters more than most people realize.

When someone comes to you with a problem, the instinct is to solve it. You have been where they are. You have made the mistake they are about to make. You can see the answer from where you are standing, and it would take you thirty seconds to just say it. So you do.

And then you wonder why nothing changes.

The answer you give belongs to you. The person across from you nods, says thank you, and walks away holding your solution in their hands. But they did not build it. They do not fully trust it. And the next time a similar problem shows up wearing a slightly different face, they are back in your office again.

That is not mentoring. That is dependency with good intentions.

Real mentoring is slower and more uncomfortable. It means sitting with someone while they think, instead of thinking for them. It means asking what do you actually want here before jumping to how do you get there. It means noticing when the problem they brought you is not the real problem, and saying so.

The best question I was ever asked by a mentor was not about strategy or career paths. It was: what are you most afraid of in this situation. I did not have an answer ready. I had to sit with it for a few days. When the answer finally surfaced, I understood the situation completely differently, and I did not need anyone to tell me what to do next.

That is the point. A good question does not just give you information. It changes how you see the problem. It hands ownership back to the person who has to live with the outcome.

Here is the part for whoever is doing the mentoring.

You are not there to be the smartest person in the conversation. You are there to make the other person think more clearly than they could on their own. Those are completely different jobs.

Some things that actually help. Ask what have you already tried before offering anything. Ask what your gut says even when the person is convinced they have no gut feeling. Ask what would you tell a friend in this exact situation, because people are almost always more insightful about others than about themselves.

And be honest when you do not know. One of the most useful things a mentor ever said to me was I have no idea, but here is how I would think through it. That was more valuable than a polished answer would have been. It showed me a process I could use the next time they were not in the room.

There is also a trap around validation. Sometimes people come to a mentor not for direction but for permission. They already know what they want to do. They need someone to say it is okay. There is nothing wrong with that. Recognizing it is part of the job. If someone has already made up their mind and just needs a little courage, give them the courage. Do not drag them through a Socratic exercise when what they needed was a push.

The skill is knowing the difference.

Mentoring gets misunderstood as a favor you do for someone junior. I think of it differently. Every good question you ask sharpens your own thinking. Every time you have to explain why something works without just demonstrating it, you understand it better yourself. The mentoring relationship is not one-directional, even when it looks that way from the outside.

If you are in a position where people come to you for guidance, the most useful thing you can do is resist the urge to talk first. Ask one question. Wait. See where they go with it. You might be surprised how close they already are to the answer.

They usually do not need you to hand it to them. They need you to help them see that they already have it.

· Abdallah

Currently on both sides of this, asking questions I wish someone had asked me sooner.

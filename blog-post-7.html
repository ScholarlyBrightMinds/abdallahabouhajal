The Meeting That Should Have Been a Message

You know the feeling. You finally get into the work, the real work, the kind where an hour disappears and you come out the other side with something actual. Then a calendar invite lands and takes the rest of the afternoon.

Not because the meeting was long. Because the anticipation of it was.

That is the tax nobody puts on the agenda. A one-hour meeting at 3pm does not cost one hour. It costs everything after lunch, because your brain knows it cannot commit to anything deep before then. You putter. You answer emails. You tell yourself you are warming up. What you are actually doing is waiting.

Most meetings are not bad because of what happens inside them. They are bad because of what they displace.

When a meeting actually earns its place

A message handles information. A meeting handles people. That is the line I keep coming back to.

If you are sharing an update, a decision that has already been made, a document that needs to be read, that is a message. Write it. Send it. Let people read it on their own time and respond when their brain is ready. They will give you a better answer than if you had pulled them into a room and waited for them to form an opinion on the spot.

But when something is genuinely tangled, when you need to feel whether two people actually agree or are just being polite, when the back-and-forth of a real conversation would unlock something in thirty minutes that would take three days over email, that is when a meeting earns its place. Those exist. They matter. I am not arguing against them.

I am arguing for being honest about which kind you are calling.

Most status updates are messages wearing a meeting's clothes. Most brainstorms are one person talking and four people waiting for it to end. Most check-ins exist because someone did not trust that silence means progress.

So before you send the invite, ask what would happen if you wrote it out instead. If the answer is nothing, write it out.

The other side of this

There is a version of protecting your focus that tips into something less useful. You start declining things. Then you start declining anything that could have been a message, which sounds principled until you realize you have optimized yourself out of the loop. People stop including you. Decisions get made without the context you had. You were deep in your work, but the work slowly stopped mattering to the people around it.

Deep work needs protection. It also needs to be connected to something.

The move is not to decline meetings. It is to make the ones you attend count, to be present enough that people do not need to call you as often, and to build enough trust that when you do say you will read the brief instead, they believe you actually will.

Block the mornings if that is when you think best. Batch the meetings into afternoons. Ask for an agenda before you accept. These are small habits, and none of them require being difficult.

If you are the one sending the calendar invites, sit with the question for a moment before you hit send. Who is this for, really? What do you need from these people that a written message cannot give you? If you have a clear answer, great. Book the meeting. If the answer is fuzzy, that is usually a sign.

Your attention is the one resource you cannot get back at the end of the quarter. Spend it accordingly.

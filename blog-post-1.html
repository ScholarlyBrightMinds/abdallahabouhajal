Just Thinking

Should You Go the Distance?

Oct 14, 2025

I’ve been thinking about what it really means to “go the distance.” In research, the finish line is never a straight line—especially when you live between two worlds: pharmacy and AI for drug discovery. One is slow, careful, and clinical. The other is fast, experimental, and sometimes brutally wrong before it’s right.

I used to believe distance was about raw effort. Lately I think it’s mostly about direction and compounding. A tiny, well-aimed step you repeat every day outperforms a heroic sprint in the wrong direction.

My simple rule

Distance = Focus × Consistency × Feedback

  • Focus: Fewer projects, deeper work. Say no to the noise.
  • Consistency: Show up—even when the model misbehaves, the assay fails, or the idea looks naïve.
  • Feedback: Papers, mentors, reviewers, or users—close the loop quickly and often.

Where I’m pointing that energy

  • Bridging AI and bench reality: Models that respect chemistry and clinical context, not just metrics.
  • Writing and editing: Clear research is a form of service. Good science deserves good storytelling.
  • Learning in public: Notes, small demos, and honest post-mortems over polished but distant “someday.”
The work gets easier when the path gets narrower.

Small bets I trust

  • Read one paper deeply; write a 150-word summary in plain English.
  • Prototype a tiny model; document one failure and one lesson.
  • Reach out to a researcher you admire with a specific question.
  • Edit something: a draft, a figure, a paragraph. Make it kinder and clearer.

Will I go the distance? I don’t know. But I’m learning that distance isn’t a dramatic leap—just a thousand honest steps in a direction that still excites you after the novelty burns off. Today, that direction is useful AI for drug discovery and clearer scientific communication. Tomorrow, I’ll take another step.

— Abdallah
